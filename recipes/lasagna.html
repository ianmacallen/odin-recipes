Sauce for Filling

Ingredients

  • 1/2 lb beef
  • 1/2 lb pork
  • parsley
  • 2 garlic cloves
  • olive oil
  • 4 oz butter
  • 2 oz pancetta
  • 3/4 lb onions
  • salt
  • pepper
  • 24 oz tomatos
  • 1 green pepper
  • chopped meatballs
  • 3 oz toamto paste

Instructions

  1. Brown pancetta in butter and oil.
  2. Add onions, garlic and parrsley
  3. Add ground meat
  4. Salt and pepper
  5. Add tomatoes
  6. Add green pepper
  7. Simmer for abotu 20 minutes
  8. Add meat balls
  9. Simmer for 90 minutes
  10. Add tomato paste and stir.

Sauce for Topping

Ingredients

  • 2 tablespoons butter
  • 2 tablespoons cream
  • 1 cup toamto sauce
  • 2 tablespoons flour

Instructions

  1. Combine butter and cream
  2. Heat gently
  3. Add tomatoes
  4. Stir in flour

Lasagna

Ingredients

  • Filling sauce
  • Topping sauce
  • Cooked lasagna noodles
  • 2 lbs ricotta
  • Meat Balls
  • 2 cups Parmesan

Instructions

  1. Add layer of filling s auce
  2. Layer in three noodles
  3. Spread 1 lb ricotta
  4. Add some meat balls
  5. Add some Parmesan
  6. Add sauce and repeat layer
  7. Cover with a layer of noodles
  8. Spoon tooping sauce over noodles
  9. Cover and bake for 20 minutes at 350f

Who Was Mama Leone?

Yes, there was a real Mama Leone. She founded the restaurant in the early 1900s. Eventually her son Gene took over before selling the restaurant in the post-war period.

Mama Leone's started out as a "living room" restaurant in the house of the owner. Eventually the restuarant moved north from the west village of Manhattan to midtown, where 1,200 diners could be served at once across multiple dining rooms. Eventually, the restuarant was sold to Restaraunt Associates, a conglomerate with experience running airport restaurants at Newark Airport.

Red Sauce: How Italian Food Became American

If you love Italian-American food, you'll love Red Sauce: How Italian Food Became American. There is a whole chapter talking about lasgana noodles.

The book traces the evolution of traditional Italian-American cuisine, often referred to as “red sauce Italian,” from its origins in Italy to its transformation in America into a new, distinct cuisine. It is a fascinating social and culinary history exploring the integration of red sauce food into mainstream America alongside the blending of Italian immigrant otherness into a national American identity. The story follows the small parlor restaurants immigrants launched from their homes to large, popular destinations, and eventually to commodified fast food and casual dining restaurants. Some dishes like fettuccine Alfredo and spaghetti alla Caruso owe their success to celebrities, and Italian-American cuisine generally has benefitted from a rich history in popular culture.
